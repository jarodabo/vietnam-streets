Flavors of Home

Chef Linh Nguyen brings her grandmother's recipes to life in Orange County, bridging two cultures through food and memory.

Westminster, CA
December 2024
6 min read

In Little Saigon, a young chef transforms her grandmother's handwritten recipes into a thriving restaurant, preserving family history one bowl of phở at a time.

Linh Nguyen's grandmother never measured ingredients. She cooked by feel, by memory, by the wisdom of decades spent perfecting each dish. When Bà Ngoại passed away in 2020, she left behind a weathered notebook filled with recipes written in Vietnamese—cryptic notes like "a little bit of this" and "until it feels right."

For Linh, a second-generation Vietnamese-American who grew up speaking English, that notebook became both a challenge and a mission. "I barely read Vietnamese," she admits, standing in the kitchen of her Westminster restaurant. "But these recipes? They're my connection to her. To Vietnam. To who I am."

Over two years, Linh worked to decode and recreate her grandmother's dishes. She called aunts in Vietnam at odd hours, asking questions about techniques she'd only watched as a child. She tested each recipe dozens of times, trying to capture not just the flavors, but the memories—Sunday mornings filled with the aroma of cà phê sữa đá, Tết celebrations centered around her grandmother's imperial rolls.

"I'm not just cooking Vietnamese food. I'm cooking *my* Vietnamese food—the version my grandmother made for our family, flavored with her story of surviving war, building a new life in America, and never forgetting where she came from."

— Linh Nguyen, Chef & Owner

Her restaurant, Nhà Bà (Grandmother's House), opened in Little Saigon in 2023. The menu features dishes many Vietnamese-Americans grew up with but rarely find in restaurants: bò kho slow-cooked for eight hours, bún riêu made from her grandmother's precise crab-paste recipe, chè with the exact ratio of sweet and creamy Bà Ngoại perfected over decades.

But running a Vietnamese restaurant in Little Saigon—arguably the heart of Vietnamese-America—comes with pressure. Older customers, many of them refugees who fled Vietnam decades ago, have strong opinions about authenticity. "Some people tell me my phở isn't traditional enough," Linh says. "Others say it tastes exactly like their grandmother's. Both can be true."

What makes Nhà Bà special isn't perfect adherence to some mythical "authentic" Vietnamese cuisine. It's the way Linh has created space for multiple truths: that Vietnamese food in America will inevitably evolve, that diaspora identity is complex and valid, that honoring tradition doesn't mean refusing to adapt.

On any given evening, you'll find three generations sitting together at Nhà Bà: elderly Vietnamese refugees sharing memories of Saigon, their American-born children navigating two cultures, and their grandchildren who might not speak Vietnamese but find connection through food. At the table next to them might be a young Vietnamese-American couple on their first date, or a white family trying Vietnamese food for the first time, guided by Linh's careful explanations.

Linh keeps her grandmother's notebook in a shadow box on the restaurant wall. It's a reminder that food carries memory, that recipes can be love letters across generations and oceans, and that the work of cultural preservation happens not in museums, but in kitchens—in the daily act of cooking, sharing, and remembering.
